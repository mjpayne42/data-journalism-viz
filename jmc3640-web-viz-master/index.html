Who's contributing to the political-campaign committees of Iowa lawmakers representing Johnson County?

Candidates seeking to represent Johnson County residents in the Iowa Senate overall see more variance in the source of their political-campaign committee's contributions than do state House candidates, and have more money funneled into their campaigns, Iowa campaign-finance records show. State Sen. Kevin Kinney, D-Oxford, reported the highest amount of campaign contributions received from committees — party-affiliated groups, political-action committees, or other institutional supporters — totaling around 84 percent. Sen. Zach Wahls, D-Coralville, showed the opposite trend, with his committee raising more than 95 percent of his contributions from individuals. Sen. Joe Bolkcom, D-Iowa City, received roughly half of his contributions from individuals and half from committees, with a slight edge in contributions from individuals. Both Kinney and Bolkcom faced competition from Republican candidates in the 2018 general election, while Wahls did not face off against another major-party candidate in the general. He ran against a Libertarian candidate.

House candidates raised less in funding and faced less competition in their races, with each candidate being an incumbent. Most House candidates received around or less than $50,000 with the exception of Rep. Bobby Kaufmann, R-Wilton. That compares to the lowest total among the Senate candidates of $172,882 raised by Bolkcom. Kaufmann ran against Democrat Jodi Clemens in the 2018 midterms, and his margin of victory came in at around an 11% lead — about 1,600 votes more. He was one of two House candidates, along with Rep. Dave Jacoby, D-Coralville, whose campaign committee received more in contributions from committees rather than individuals. Additionally, Kaufmann was the only candidate to run as a Republican and to run in a competitive election. The other House candidates ran uncontested.

Methodology

My first step was collecting data from the seven state lawmakers representing Johnson County, narrowing down specific dates of campaign contributions in my search. For state senators, who serve four-year terms, I dated my search to align with the first reporting period after their terms and the last reporting period before the general election. While Sen. Kevin Kinney, D-Oxford, and Sen. Joe Bolkcom, D-Iowa City, were incumbents, Sen. Zach Wahls, D-Coralville, had just launched his bid for his Senate district in 2017, meaning he had less time to fundraise. I used the same method for state representatives, but they serve two-year terms, which is a factor of why they received less in contributions than senators.
